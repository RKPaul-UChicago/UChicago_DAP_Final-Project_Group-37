Who Votes in Chicago?
Participation and Representativeness in Presidential Elections, 2008-2024

Motivation and Research Question

Democratic systems rely on broad participation in elections, yet voter turnout rarely occurs evenly across communities. In cities like Chicago, participation varies widely across neighborhoods with different socioeconomic conditions. These differences matter because turnout ultimately shapes whose preferences are represented in democratic decision-making. When some communities consistently participate at higher rates than others, the electorate may not fully reflect the population as a whole.

This project examines who votes in Chicago and how representative the electorate is across neighborhoods during presidential elections from 2008 through 2024. Specifically, we ask two related questions. First, how does voter turnout vary spatially across Chicago precincts? Second, how strongly are turnout patterns associated with neighborhood socioeconomic and demographic characteristics?

Understanding these patterns has practical implications for policymakers, civic organizations, and election administrators. If participation gaps are concentrated in particular types of neighborhoods, targeted policies may help reduce barriers to participation and strengthen democratic representation. By analyzing turnout across five presidential elections that capture varying political contexts, this project provides a long-term perspective on how participation patterns persist across time and how they relate to structural neighborhood characteristics.

Our goal is not to establish causal relationships but to provide a clear descriptive policy-oriented evidence of turnout inequality across Chicago and explore what these patterns imply for civic engagement and public policy.

Data and Methodology

To analyze voting behavior across the city, we combine precinct-level election results with demographic and spatial data from several sources.

First, we use voter turnout data from the Chicago Board of Elections, which provide counts of registered voters and ballots cast for each precinct during presidential elections from 2008 to 2024. These data allow us to calculate turnout rates for thousands of precinct observations across five election cycles.

Second, we incorporate neighborhood demographic characteristics from the American Community Survey (ACS). We use ACS 5-year estimates aligned to each election cycle to approximate neighborhood conditions during that period. The demographic variables we focus on include median household income, college education, renter share, racial composition, and the share of young residents (age 18–29), all measured at the census tract level. These variables capture important socioeconomic conditions that may influence voter participation. To ensure the analysis focuses strictly on the city, ACS census tracts are clipped to the Chicago municipal boundary, removing areas outside city limits.

Third, we use geographic boundary files for both census tracts and voting precincts. Precinct boundary files are obtained from the Chicago Data Portal, while census tract shapefiles come from the U.S. Census TIGER/Line program. Because these geographic units do not share identical boundaries, demographic information cannot be merged directly onto precinct-level election data. To address this challenge, we apply area-weighted spatial interpolation, which calculates the geographic overlap between census tracts and precinct polygons and assigns demographic characteristics to precincts proportionally based on their overlapping area.The resulting dataset is a precinct-by-year panel, where each observation represents a precinct in a particular election year and includes turnout rates, demographic characteristics, and geographic identifiers. To summarize neighborhood conditions more clearly, we construct a Socioeconomic Status (SES) index using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). This index combines several demographic characteristics into a single measure capturing overall neighborhood socioeconomic advantage.Together, the final master data allow us to examine how turnout varies spatially across Chicago and how strongly participation is associated with neighborhood characteristics.

Analysis Approach and Dashboard

We combine static figures with an interactive dashboard to examine spatial turnout patterns, the relationship between turnout and socioeconomic status, and demographic correlations across election years.

Key Findings

Spatial Patterns in Voter Turnout

Our first visualization maps turnout rates across Chicago precincts. The maps reveal clear and persistent spatial patterns in voter participation. Higher-turnout precincts consistently appear in parts of the North Side and lakefront neighborhoods, while lower turnout is more common in parts of the South and West sides of the city. Importantly, these spatial patterns remain visible across all five election years, illustrating that turnout differences are geographically clustered rather than randomly distributed across the city. Even in high-turnout presidential elections (2008 and 2012), relative differences between neighborhoods remain strikingly stable. This suggests that turnout inequality is not driven solely by specific candidates or campaign mobilization. Instead, it reflects deeper structural differences across neighborhoods.

Socioeconomic Status (SES) vs. Voter Turnout

To explore whether neighborhood socioeconomic conditions are associated with voter participation, we examine the relationship between turnout and the SES index.The scatter plots show a strong positive relationship between socioeconomic status and turnout. Precincts with higher SES scores tend to exhibit higher turnout rates, while precincts with lower SES scores generally show lower participation.

This relationship appears in every election cycle in our dataset. While turnout increases during highly competitive elections, the relative gap between high-SES and low-SES precincts remains visible.These results suggest that socioeconomic conditions are strongly associated with participation. Neighborhood characteristics such as income levels, educational attainment, and age composition likely shape both the resources and institutional familiarity that facilitate voter engagement.

To quantify the above relationship more formally, we estimate a two-way fixed effects panel regression that controls for both precinct-level and election-year differences. The regression results also indicate a positive and statistically significant relationship between socioeconomic status and turnout. Substantively, the estimates suggest that if a precinct becomes one standard deviation more socioeconomically advantaged, turnout increases by approximately 3.3 percentage points. Tne results reinforce the descriptive patterns observed in the visualizations.

Demographic Patterns in Turnout

Finally, we examine how turnout correlates with several demographic characteristics across election cycles. The heatmap summarizes the Spearman correlation between precinct turnout rates and key demographic variables for each election year. Several patterns emerge clearly. Income and education show the strongest positive relationships with turnout across election cycles. Precincts with higher household incomes and larger shares of college-educated residents consistently exhibit higher participation rates. By contrast, several demographic characteristics are negatively associated with turnout. Precincts with higher shares of renters and younger residents tend to exhibit lower participation rates. Similarly, the share of Black residents shows a negative relationship with turnout in most election years, particularly in more recent elections. Overall, the heatmap confirms that turnout differences across Chicago are strongly associated with structural neighborhood characteristics.

Policy Implications and Recommendations

Education emerges as the most consistent predictor of turnout differences, suggesting that turnout gaps may reflect differences in civic knowledge, access to political information, and familiarity with voting institutions rather than purely financial constraints. The findings suggest that voter outreach should be more targeted toward lower-SES precincts where participation gaps are largest. Community-based outreach, voter information campaigns, and civic education efforts aimed at younger residents may help reduce informational barriers and strengthen long-term participation. Civic organizations, campaigns, and local agencies all have a role to play in these efforts.

Interactive Dashboard and Policy Use

In addition to the static analysis presented in this report, we developed an interactive dashboard that allows users to explore Chicago’s voter turnout patterns across precincts and election years. This tool can support election administrators, local governments, and civic organizations in identifying neighborhoods where voter engagement initiatives may have the greatest impact. It can also help campaign strategists and mobilization groups locate areas where turnout potential appears underutilized and where targeted outreach may be most effective. Limitations

When interpreting these results, it should be considered that the analysis is descriptive rather than causal, and turnout is also influenced by factors not captured here, including campaign mobilization, candidate effects, and historical voting patterns. In addition, the area-weighted interpolation of tract demographics to precincts introduces some measurement uncertainty.

Conclusion

Despite these limitations, the analysis provides a clear picture of persistent turnout inequality across Chicago. These patterns can help policymakers and civic organizations identify where efforts to strengthen participation may be most needed.
